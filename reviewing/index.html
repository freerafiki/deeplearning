(2012) ImageNet Classification with Deep Convolutional Neural Networks

IMAGES
Link to the Paper
First big breakthrough in 2012 where the ImageNet Challenge was won by far from the neural network presented in this paper. They combined various techniques, with a network that seen now looks very trivial. They were the first to use ReLU instead of the (then) standard tanh activation function, which ensured faster training times. Plus they trained on multiple GPUs. The biggest advantage is the training, which is longer and much better organized, more than a very innovative architecture (they order the section in terms of importance, and ReLU comes first, network architecture only fifth). The contributions, in order of importance, are described as:
  1. using ReLU as activation function to reduce training time;
  2. parallelization of the training on multiple GPU with partial comunication (only in some layers);
  3. response normalization: ReLU tends to learn and increase weights, and a normalization helps improving generalization across adjacent neurons; (1.2-1.4% error reduction)
  4. overlapping pooling: the patch used to group neighbouring pixels are overlapping and this tends to reduce chances overfitting (very slightly, it seems it's not the winning factor - 0.3-0.4% error reduction);
  5. overall architecture: as said, this is the last point, which may sound counterintuitive. It has 8 layers, 5 fully convolutional and 3 fully connected layers, the last is a softmax with 1000 output neurons (for the 1000 class)
Moreover, they also took care of reducing overfitting, by using:
  • data augmentation (the idea was taken from previous work, so it's nothing new in this case, the feeling is that they did it better, or at least it worked better combined with the other features they introduced) under the form of image translation and horizontal reflections and altering the RGB intensities ( This proved to be very effective - over 1% error reduction) and
  • dropout, which at the time was a recently introduced techniques, with a probability of p=0.5.
They trained using stochastic gradient descent, gaussian initialization of the weights for all layers for five to six days at the time. Results were very good and showed that larger network could work. It was probably the first time that using a larger network worked (they even tested removing some layers to show that shallower networks did not reach such high accuracies, stating the importante of deeper networks) because of a well-structured training (and the activation function), which makes a huge difference, because it proved the potential of larger network, which then developed much further until the huge network we see now.
TL;DR
ReLU combined with longer training on multiple GPU worked as a proof of concept for the ability of larger network to reach higher accuracy on classification tasks.
